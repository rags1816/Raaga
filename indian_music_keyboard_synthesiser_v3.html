🎤 Singer's Shruti Box — Find Your Sa
Sur = Pitch · Laya = Rhythm · Taan = Melodic Speed · Taal = Meter. A singer must first find their natural Sa (root pitch) before beginning practice.

How to Find Your Natural Sa
Step 1: Sing "Aaaaaa" comfortably for 30 seconds without strain.
Step 2: Click each pitch button. The note where you feel most relaxed — that's your Sa.
Step 3: From your Sa, sing Pa (5th). If comfortable, you've found it. If strained, try a lower Sa.
Step 4: Anchor with Tanpura Drone (in the Instrument Lab). Practice 10 min daily.

Male voice: Typically G3, Ab3, A3, or Bb3 · Female voice: Typically C4, D4, or E4 (261–330 Hz)

🎵 Sur — Pitch Mastery

Being "besura" (off-pitch) is the first thing an Indian classical teacher corrects. The shruti box (this tool) is essential. Sing Sa daily while listening to the reference — over time your ear internalises the exact pitch. This is called shruti gyan (knowledge of microtones).

⏱ Laya — Rhythm Flow

Laya is more than tempo — it's the inner pulse. Vilambit laya allows space for ornaments and emotion. Drut laya demands precision. A common beginner mistake: rushing at faster tempos. Practice with tabla or metronome always.

🎼 Taan — Speed Runs

Taan are rapid melodic passages through the raaga's notes. Build speed by starting at 50% tempo and gradually increasing. Use the speed control in Play-Along mode. A taan that's accurate at slow speed will naturally become fast with repetition — this takes weeks of daily practice.

🥁 Taal — The Time Cycle

Taal is the rhythmic cycle. Sam (Beat 1, ✕) is sacred — the melody and rhythm must land here together. Missing the sam is a serious error. Start by clapping the taal cycle while listening to tabla, before even touching the instrument. Feel the cycle in your body.

Music Theory — Hindustani Classical

The 10 Thaats

All raagas derive from 10 parent scales. Bilawal = pure major. Kalyan = F# (Yaman). Bhairavi = all komal notes. Kafi = komal Ga & Ni. The thaat defines the scale structure; the raaga adds grammar (which notes to emphasize, ornament, or avoid).

Comparing This App to Others

Riyaz uses AI pitch detection via microphone. iTabla Pro uses real recorded tabla samples. Tanpura Droid uses actual tanpura recordings. This app uses FluidR3_GM soundfont samples (same as GarageBand MIDI mode) — real sampled notes, not synthesis.

Veena vs Sitar

Veena (Carnatic) is a fretted plucked instrument, deeper and rounder in tone. Sitar (Hindustani) has a brighter, more metallic buzz (jawari). In this app: Veena = longer release (3.5s) giving sustained warm resonance. Both use Karplus-Strong physical string modelling.

Guitar Types Compared

Nylon string: warm, mellow — classical and Spanish. Steel string: brighter, twangy — folk and country. Electric clean: pure, sustaining — jazz and blues. Overdriven: crunchy, harmonic distortion — rock. Bass: deep, low fundamental — backbone of Western rhythm.

How to Practise Like a Music Class

1) Start Tanpura every session. 2) Sing Sa to anchor your pitch. 3) Play aaroh-avaroh of the raaga up and down slowly. 4) Learn the song in Practise mode one note at a time with Guruji correcting you. 5) Add tabla rhythm and play the song again. 6) Play-Along mode tests your timing and accuracy. This is the authentic shishya-guru learning cycle — riyaz (daily practice) is the key.

Ornaments — Gamak & Meend

Meend = smooth glide between notes. Gamak = oscillating heavy ornament. Kan = grace note. Murki = quick ornament cluster. Andolan = gentle sway on specific notes. These ornaments are what separate a mechanical note-player from a true musician — they carry the bhav (emotional expression).
