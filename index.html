How to Find Your Natural Sa
Step 1: Sing "Aaaaaa" comfortably for 30 seconds without strain.
Step 2: Click each pitch button. The note where you feel most relaxed — that's your Sa.
Step 3: From your Sa, sing Pa (5th). If comfortable, you've found it. If strained, try a lower Sa.
Step 4: Anchor with Tanpura Drone (in the Instrument Lab). Practice 10 min daily.

Male voice: Typically G3, Ab3, A3, or Bb3 · Female voice: Typically C4, D4, or E4 (261–330 Hz)

🎵 Sur — Pitch Mastery

Being "besura" (off-pitch) is the first thing an Indian classical teacher corrects. The shruti box (this tool) is essential. Sing Sa daily while listening to the reference — over time your ear internalises the exact pitch. This is called shruti gyan (knowledge of microtones).

⏱ Laya — Rhythm Flow

Laya is more than tempo — it's the inner pulse. Vilambit laya allows space for ornaments and emotion. Drut laya demands precision. A common beginner mistake: rushing at faster tempos. Practice with tabla or metronome always.

🎼 Taan — Speed Runs

Taan are rapid melodic passages through the raaga's notes. Build speed by starting at 50% tempo and gradually increasing. Use the speed control in Play-Along mode. A taan that's accurate at slow speed will naturally become fast with repetition — this takes weeks of daily practice.

🥁 Taal — The Time Cycle

Taal is the rhythmic cycle. Sam (Beat 1, ✕) is sacred — the melody and rhythm must land here together. Missing the sam is a serious error. Start by clapping the taal cycle while listening to tabla, before even touching the instrument. Feel the cycle in your body.

Music Theory — Hindustani Classical

The 10 Thaats

All raagas derive from 10 parent scales. Bilawal = pure major. Kalyan = F# (Yaman). Bhairavi = all komal notes. Kafi = komal Ga & Ni. The thaat defines scale structure; the raaga adds grammar — which notes to emphasise, ornament, or avoid.

Vadi, Samvadi & Pakad

Every raaga has a Vadi (most important note, most emphasised) and Samvadi (second most important, consonant with vadi). The Pakad is the characteristic phrase that identifies the raaga even from a few notes. Film composers use these instinctively.

Aaroh, Avaroh & Octave

Aaroh = ascending scale of the raaga. Avaroh = descending — often different notes. Saptak = one octave. Mandra = lower octave (Z–M keys). Madhya = middle octave (A–K keys). Taar = upper octave. Sa and Pa are achal — never altered in any raaga.

Veena vs Sitar

Veena (Carnatic): fretted, deeper, warmer tone, longer sustain. Used in South Indian classical. Sitar (Hindustani): brighter, metallic buzz called jawari from the curved bridge. In this app: Veena has 4.5s release vs Sitar's 2.8s — you can hear the difference clearly.

Guitar Types Compared

Nylon string: warm, mellow — classical & Spanish. Steel string: brighter, twangy — folk & country. Electric clean: pure, sustaining — jazz & blues. Overdriven: crunchy, harmonic distortion — rock. Bass: deep, low fundamental — backbone of Western rhythm.

What is BPM & Laya?

BPM (Beats Per Minute) = how fast the taal cycles. 90 BPM = Madhya (medium). 48 BPM = Vilambit (slow) — gives space for ornaments. 140+ BPM = Drut (fast). In Indian music Laya is more than tempo — it is the inner pulse felt in the body. Always learn at Vilambit first.

Taal, Sam & Vibhag

Taal = rhythmic time cycle. Sam (×) = beat 1, always the strongest — the melody must land here. Khali (0) = empty beat, played quietly. Vibhag = sub-division of the taal. Keherva = 4+4 (8 beats). Teental = 4+4+4+4 (16 beats). Feel the taal by clapping before playing.

How to Practise Like a Music Class

1) Start Tanpura every session. 2) Sing Sa to anchor pitch. 3) Play aaroh-avaroh of the raaga slowly. 4) Learn the song in Practise mode — Guruji corrects every mistake. 5) Add tabla rhythm and play the song again. 6) Play-Along tests your timing. This is the authentic shishya-guru cycle. Daily riyaz (practice) is the only path.

Ornaments — Gamak & Meend

Meend = smooth glide between notes. Gamak = oscillating heavy ornament. Kan = grace note. Murki = quick ornament cluster. Andolan = gentle sway on specific notes. These separate a mechanical player from a musician — they carry the bhav (emotional expression).

Sargam Notation Guide

Capital letter = shuddh (natural): S R G M P D N. Lowercase = komal (flat): r g d n. m = tivra Ma (sharp F#). Dot above = upper octave (Ṡ). Dot below = lower octave. Dash (–) = hold the note. This is the universal written language of Indian classical music.
